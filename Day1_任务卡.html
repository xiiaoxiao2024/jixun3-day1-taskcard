Group A

New Kid's First Day

Mia stood by the classroom door. She felt nervous. It was her first day at a new school.

Everyone looked at her. Mia felt shy. She wanted to hide behind her backpack.

"Hi! I'm Ben," said a boy with a cheerful smile. He was friendly and kind.

Mia felt relieved. Maybe this school would not be so bad.

At lunch, a girl named Zoe waved. "Come sit with us!" she said.

Mia was still shy, but she walked over. Zoe was polite and asked Mia lots of questions.

By the end of the day, Mia felt proud. She had made two new friends.

She was no longer lonely. She felt excited for tomorrow.

Group A

The Lost Backpack

Sam couldn't find his backpack anywhere. He felt worried. His homework was inside!

"Did you check under your bed?" asked his sister. She was always calm, even when Sam was not.

Sam looked. No backpack. He felt more scared. What if he lost it forever?

Then Sam remembered — he left it at the park! He felt silly for forgetting.

Sam ran to the park. His backpack was still on the bench. He felt so happy.

"I'm glad I was careful and checked the bench first," he said.

His sister smiled. "See? No need to be so worried next time!"

Sam felt grateful that his backpack was safe. He promised to be less careless next time.

Group B
Group B

A Misunderstanding That Hurt

Jake was angry when he saw his favorite book missing from his desk. He was sure that Sam, his clumsy best friend, had taken it without asking.

All day, Jake gave Sam confused looks and refused to talk to him. Sam looked worried and hurt, but he stayed patient, hoping Jake would explain what was wrong.

After school, Jake found the book — it had fallen behind his own bookshelf the whole time. He felt embarrassed and disappointed in himself for blaming his loyal friend.

"I'm sorry," Jake said, his voice quiet. "I was so quick to think the worst."

Sam, who was naturally generous and caring, forgave him right away. "It's okay," he said with a gentle smile. "Everyone makes mistakes when they're nervous."

Jake felt grateful that Sam was so understanding. He promised to be more careful before jumping to conclusions next time.

Group B

The Group Project

Zoe, Marcus, and Ellie were working on a group project, but nothing was going right.

Marcus was bossy, telling everyone exactly what to do. Ellie felt annoyed and stayed quiet, drawing instead of talking.

Zoe felt tired of the arguing. She was usually calm, but today she felt frustrated too.

"Can we just talk about this like a team?" Zoe asked, trying to stay patient.

Marcus looked surprised. He didn't realize he had been so bossy. "Sorry," he said, looking embarrassed. "I guess I was being selfish with the ideas."

Ellie, who was more talkative than she seemed, finally spoke up with a clever idea that combined everyone's plans.

They all felt hopeful again. Working together, careful and determined, they finished the project — and it turned out to be their best one yet.

Group C
Group C

The Feeling She Couldn't Say Out Loud

Priya sat in the back row, watching her best friend Naomi finish yet another flawless piano piece. The applause was thunderous, and Priya felt something twist uncomfortably in her chest — she was jealous, though she hated to admit it.

It wasn't that Priya wasn't proud of Naomi. She was. But being endlessly compared to someone so effortlessly talented made her feel tired of trying, and if she was honest, a little bit selfish for wanting the spotlight too.

That evening, Naomi noticed Priya's quiet mood and asked what was wrong. Priya hesitated, embarrassed by how silly her feelings sounded out loud. But Naomi, ever thoughtful and caring, didn't laugh or judge.

"I get jealous of you too, sometimes," Naomi admitted quietly. "You're so confident on stage. I'm always fearful before I play."

Priya was surprised. She had never imagined her graceful, composed friend felt anything but calm. Somehow, the honest confession made Priya's envy melt into something more like gratitude — and determined admiration.

Group C

The Talent Show

Backstage, Leo's hands wouldn't stop shaking. In ten minutes, he would walk onto that stage and play his violin in front of the entire school — something he'd been dreading and secretly hoping for all year.

His best friend, Amara, sat beside him, unusually quiet for someone so talkative. "You're going to be fine," she said, though her own voice sounded a little fearful too; she still remembered how nervous she'd felt during her own performance last year.

Leo wasn't sure if he believed her. He felt foolish for being this worried over a three-minute song. But he also felt determined — he had practiced too hard to back out now.

When his name was called, something shifted. He walked out, calm and confident despite the trembling in his fingers, and by the final note, the whole auditorium was on its feet.

Amara ran over first, grinning. "See? I told you," she said, more proud of him than she'd expected to feel. Leo, still a little dazed, just laughed — grateful, relieved, and already a little less fearful about what came next.
